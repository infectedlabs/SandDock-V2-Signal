## What is Cornix and how does it work?

Cornix is an automated execution tool that connects Telegram signal groups to crypto exchanges via API. It automatically parses text-based trading signals posted in channels and places corresponding market or limit orders on the user's exchange account.

Unlike platform-native trading bots or proprietary signal generators, Cornix does not produce trading strategies itself. Instead, it functions as a technical bridge. To use Cornix, you must configure API keys on your cryptocurrency exchange (such as Binance, Bybit, or OKX) with trading permissions enabled, and then link these keys to your Cornix account. When a signal channel you subscribe to publishes a signal, Cornix's parser reads the entry price, take-profit targets, and stop-loss levels, executing them according to your custom risk settings.

This execution-focused design is powerful for traders who have already found highly reliable signal groups and want hands-free execution. However, it places the entire burden of configuration, API security, and signal selection on the individual trader.

## Why do traders search for a Cornix alternative?

Traders often seek Cornix alternatives due to three core pain points: complex API configuration, fee stacking across multiple paid subscriptions, and strict refund policies with minimal support responsiveness. These issues make automated signal execution expensive and high-risk.

While automation promises convenience, the reality of managing API keys, connection dropouts, and exchange rate limits is complex. When an API connection fails, trades may go unexecuted, or worse, open positions may be left without active stop-losses. This has led many traders to re-evaluate whether automated execution is worth the potential security risks and technical overhead, especially when compared to simpler, manual alert systems.

Additionally, because Cornix relies entirely on third-party inputs, users are forced to manage multiple subscriptions. If the signals they subscribe to underperform, the trader still bears the cost of the execution software, the signal channel, and the exchange trading fees. This dynamic has driven a shift toward unified signal platforms that handle analysis in-house.

## How does fee stacking affect Cornix users?

Fee stacking occurs because Cornix is only an execution bridge, requiring users to pay separately for both the Cornix bot subscription and the premium signal channels. When exchange trading fees are added, the total monthly cost can quickly exceed trading profits.

To understand the financial impact of fee stacking, consider the typical breakdown of costs for a Cornix user:
1. **The Execution Fee**: A paid monthly subscription to Cornix (typically ranging from $15 to $59+ depending on features like trailing stops and concurrent trades).
2. **The Signal Fee**: Subscription fees for premium Telegram channels, which frequently cost between $30 and $150 per month.
3. **Exchange Fees**: Trading fees (maker/taker) charged by the exchange on every executed position, including entries, multiple take-profits, and stop-losses.

For a retail trader operating with a small to mid-sized bankroll (e.g., $1,000 to $5,000), these stacked fixed costs create a significant hurdle. A trader might need to generate a 10% to 20% return every single month just to break even on subscription costs before seeing any actual net profit. If a signal provider has a losing month, the trader still loses money on trades while paying the full price for both the signal channel and Cornix.

## What are the main refund complaints against Cornix?

A recurring complaint in the Cornix community centers on the platform's strict refund policy, where subscriptions auto-renew without warning and refunds are routinely denied. Many users report frustration when trying to retrieve funds after experiencing technical connection issues.

Public review platforms like Trustpilot highlight persistent dissatisfaction with Cornix’s billing practices. Users frequently complain about:
- **Auto-Renewal Settings**: Subscriptions renewing automatically without proactive email notifications, catching users off guard.
- **Refund Denials**: A rigid refund policy that does not offer partial or prorated refunds, even if the user cancels within hours of an automatic renewal.
- **Technical Dropouts as Refund Rationale**: Users who experience persistent exchange connection issues or API parsing errors often feel they are paying for a service they cannot technically use, yet customer support routinely denies refund requests in these scenarios, citing their terms of service.

This commercial friction has made transparency a key requirement for traders looking for alternative services.
